Taiwan at a Glance

Key Facts

Economic Powerhouse:

  • GDP: $790B (Switzerland-sized economy)
  • GDP per capita: $33,775
  • Major trading partner

Democratic Leader:

  • Ranked #8 globally in democracy
  • Free and fair elections
  • Strong civil liberties

Despite its small size (Maryland-sized), Taiwan has achieved remarkable development.

Sources: World Bank (2024), Taiwan National Statistics, EIU Democracy Index 2024, CIA World Factbook

Taiwan is a prosperous democracy with 23.5 million people, comparable to Florida. Despite its small size, it has achieved remarkable economic development. Taiwan ranks 8th globally in the Democracy Index (Economist Intelligence Unit 2024).

Historical Context: End of WWII

Historical Context: The Chinese Civil War (1945-1949)

The Retreat to Taiwan

  • 1945: End of WWII, Japanese surrender
  • 1946-1949: Civil War resumes
  • 1949: Communist victory
    • Mao establishes PRC (Oct 1)
    • Chiang retreats to Taiwan
  • 1950s: Two governments claim legitimacy

The “Two Chinas” Question

The Dual Claim

Both Beijing and Taipei claim to be the legitimate government of all China.

PRC (People’s Republic):

  • Controls mainland China
  • Holds UN seat (since 1971)
  • 180+ countries recognize
  • “One China” policy enforced

ROC (Republic of China):

  • Governs Taiwan
  • Lost UN seat in 1971
  • Only 12 diplomatic allies
  • De facto independent

Both claim the same territory, but only one has international recognition. This creates unique diplomatic complexity.

Analysis based on official recognition, UN membership, territorial control

Both Beijing and Taipei claim to be the legitimate government of all China. The PRC controls the mainland and has international recognition; the ROC governs Taiwan but lacks formal diplomatic ties with most nations.

Taiwan’s Diplomatic Isolation

Source: Taiwan Ministry of Foreign Affairs; Council on Foreign Relations (Council on Foreign Relations 2024)

Steady Decline

From 68 countries in 1970 to just 12 today.

Key Turning Point:

  • Lost UN seat (1971)
  • PRC recognition surge

Recent Losses:

  • Honduras (2023)
  • Nicaragua (2021)
  • Panama, El Salvador (2018)

Beijing’s Pressure:

  • Economic incentives
  • Political coercion
  • “One China” enforcement

Despite isolation, Taiwan maintains robust informal ties with major economies including the U.S., Japan, and EU.

Taiwan’s formal diplomatic recognition has declined dramatically since losing its UN seat in 1971. Today only 12 nations maintain official relations, mostly small states in Latin America and the Pacific. Recent analyses show this isolation is increasingly weaponized by Beijing (Glaser 2024).

Evolution of U.S. Policy: Three Pillars

Policy Foundation

Three Documents:

1972 Shanghai Communiqué

  • Acknowledges “One China”
  • Does not endorse PRC claim

1979 Taiwan Relations Act

  • Legal basis for unofficial ties
  • Security commitments
  • Most important pillar

1982 Six Assurances

  • No arms sales termination
  • No PRC consultation on Taiwan
  • Sovereign decisions respected

Strategic Ambiguity: Deliberate vagueness on defense commitment keeps all sides cautious.

Recent shifts toward greater clarity on U.S. support.

Key policy milestones shaping U.S.-Taiwan relations

U.S. policy rests on three documents that create framework for unofficial but robust ties. Taiwan Relations Act remains most significant, providing legal basis for arms sales and security cooperation.

Taiwan’s Semiconductor Dominance

Critical Dependency

Taiwan controls over 65% of global chip manufacturing.

TSMC Dominance:

  • 58.9% global market share
  • World’s most advanced foundry
  • No close competitors

Why It Matters:

  • Every smartphone depends on Taiwan chips
  • All major tech firms are customers
  • Apple, NVIDIA, AMD, Qualcomm
  • Cannot be quickly replicated

Strategic Implications:

  • Single point of failure
  • 2-3 year lead in technology
  • Geopolitical chokepoint
  • National security concern

As Chris Miller notes in Chip War, this dominance resulted from decades of strategic investment.

Source: TrendForce Q3 2024 Foundry Market Analysis (TrendForce 2024); Miller (2022) (Miller 2022)

TSMC alone controls nearly 60% of the global foundry market. Taiwan’s combined market share exceeds 65%, making it indispensable to the global technology supply chain. As Chris Miller documents in Chip War, this dominance emerged from decades of strategic investment and specialization (Miller 2022). Recent CSIS analysis notes this creates unprecedented strategic vulnerability (Kennedy and Sutter 2024).

Advanced Chip Manufacturing: Taiwan’s Near-Monopoly

Source: BCG-SIA Report 2024 (Boston Consulting Group and Semiconductor Industry Association 2024); TSMC Annual Report 2024

Near-Monopoly on Advanced Tech

TSMC controls 92% of cutting-edge chip production (7nm and below).

Why This Matters:

  • 7nm and below = AI chips
  • Powers all major AI models
  • Required for high-performance computing
  • Smartphones, data centers

The Technology Gap:

  • TSMC: 2nm production (present 2025)
  • Samsung: 3nm (struggling)
  • Intel: 7nm (delayed multiple times)
  • China (SMIC): Stuck at 14nm+

Lead Time:

  • Minimum 2-3 years to replicate
  • Requires $20B+ investment
  • ASML equipment export restricted
  • Know-how not easily transferred

Cannot be quickly replaced.

TSMC’s dominance in advanced nodes is even more pronounced. The company controls 92% of cutting-edge production capacity - the chips that power AI, smartphones, and advanced computing. This technological lead of 2-3 years cannot be quickly overcome.

The AI Revolution: Taiwan’s Strategic Value

The AI Chokepoint

Every AI model depends on Taiwan-manufactured chips.

Supply Chain Reality:

  • AI chip design: NVIDIA, AMD, Apple
  • Manufacturing: TSMC 90%+
  • Equipment: ASML (Netherlands)
  • Applications: ChatGPT, Claude, etc.

Critical Dependency:

  • No Taiwan chips = No AI advancement
  • Training large models requires cutting-edge chips
  • Only TSMC can make them at scale

As Gregory Allen notes: “AI runs on specialized semiconductors, and those semiconductors are made in Taiwan.”

This creates both economic opportunity and strategic vulnerability.

Analysis based on: Georgetown CSET Reports (Ding and Triolo 2024); Brookings Tech Policy (Allen 2024)

AI requires cutting-edge chips that almost exclusively come from Taiwan. This creates a critical dependency: no Taiwan chips = no AI advancement. As Gregory Allen notes, “AI runs on specialized semiconductors, and those semiconductors are made in Taiwan” (Allen 2024). Recent Georgetown CSET analysis emphasizes this chokepoint represents both economic opportunity and strategic vulnerability (Ding and Triolo 2024).

U.S.-China Tech Competition

Sources: White House CHIPS Act (2022); China State Council “Made in China 2025”; CSIS China Power (Kennedy 2024)

Taiwan Caught in Middle

Both superpowers compete for technology supremacy.

U.S. Strategy:

  • Export controls on China
  • CHIPS Act ($52B investment)
  • Restrict advanced chip sales
  • Ally coordination

China Strategy:

  • “Made in China 2025”
  • Massive domestic subsidies
  • Self-sufficiency push
  • Taiwan reunification goal

Taiwan’s Position:

  • Critical to both sides
  • Pressure from Beijing
  • Security from Washington
  • Economic ties with all

Highest Tension: Taiwan stance—both see it as winner-take-all issue.

The U.S.-China tech competition centers on semiconductors, with Taiwan as the critical variable. U.S. export controls aim to maintain lead; China invests heavily in self-sufficiency. Taiwan’s stance generates maximum tension as both see control of Taiwan as decisive.

Why Taiwan Matters to the United States

Analysis framework adapted from: Bush (2021) (Bush 2021); Hass & Glaser (2024) (Hass and Glaser 2024)

Four Dimensions

U.S. interests in Taiwan are interconnected and compelling.

Economic (95):

  • Semiconductor dependency
  • $100B+ bilateral trade
  • Technology innovation hub

Strategic (90):

  • First Island Chain keystone
  • Pacific sea lanes
  • Regional power balance

Values (75):

  • Vibrant democracy
  • Human rights model
  • Self-determination

Credibility (90):

  • Allied confidence
  • Deterrence effectiveness
  • Rules-based order

Not just one reason—Taiwan matters across all dimensions simultaneously.

Taiwan’s importance to the United States spans multiple dimensions. Economic interests are highest due to semiconductors, but strategic location and credibility concerns are nearly as significant. The values dimension adds moral weight. These interests reinforce each other.

Risk Assessment: Potential Scenarios

Scenario Analysis

High Risk Quadrant:

  • Military conflict (25% prob, 100 impact)
  • Blockade (35% prob, 90 impact)
  • Accidental escalation (40% prob, 85 impact)

Most Likely:

  • Gray zone ops (85% ongoing)
  • Economic coercion (60%)
  • Cyber warfare (70%)

Critical Window:

  • 2027-2035 particularly concerning
  • PLA modernization timeline
  • Xi’s political imperatives
  • U.S. force posture gaps

Key Insight: Status quo appears stable but is actually fragile. Low-probability/high-impact events require serious preparation.

Sources: CSIS Taiwan Wargames (Cancian, Cancian, and Heginbotham 2023); RAND China Scorecard (Heginbotham et al. 2024); Expert assessments (Mastro 2024)

Risk assessment based on CSIS wargames and RAND analysis. Military conflict remains low probability but catastrophic impact. Gray zone operations and cyber warfare are ongoing. The 2027-2035 window is particularly concerning per Oriana Mastro’s analysis of PLA capabilities and Xi’s political timeline (Mastro 2024).

Risk assessments synthesized from: CSIS Taiwan Wargames (Cancian, Cancian, and Heginbotham 2023); RAND “War with China” (Heginbotham et al. 2024); DOD Indo-Pacific Strategy 2024; Expert surveys (Mastro 2024)

Expert analysis suggests gray zone operations are most probable but lower impact, while military conflict has lower probability but catastrophic consequences. CSIS wargaming exercises indicate the 2027-2035 window as particularly concerning (Cancian, Cancian, and Heginbotham 2023). Oriana Skylar Mastro notes that “Beijing’s timeline appears to be accelerating” based on PLA modernization (Mastro 2024).

Untapped Potential:

  • Shared security interests

Partnership not just defensive—also proactive and beneficial.

Engagement with Taiwan yields substantial benefits across economic, technological, and strategic dimensions. Stronger partnership could amplify these advantages while supporting democratic values. Ryan Hass argues that “deeper U.S.-Taiwan ties advance American interests across multiple domains” (Hass and Glaser 2024).

The Economic Interdependence Paradox

The Paradox

Deep economic integration makes conflict less likely yet more costly.

Trade Reality:

  • Taiwan→China: $190B (highest)
  • USA→China: $575B
  • Taiwan→USA: $110B
  • All three deeply connected

The Contradiction:

  • Economic ties create peace incentives
  • But also create vulnerability
  • Conflict would devastate all three economies
  • Yet tensions persist

Stability Through Interdependence?

  • Historical precedent mixed
  • WWI despite trade links
  • But also: EU integration worked

Key Question: Does interdependence prevent conflict or just make it more catastrophic?

Current answer: Both.

Sources: U.S. Trade Representative (2023); Taiwan Bureau of Foreign Trade; China Customs (ustr2023taiwan?)

The trilateral economic relationship creates complex interdependence. Taiwan’s $190B trade with China is its largest, creating both stability incentives and vulnerability. Economic ties make conflict costly for all parties, yet political tensions persist.

Trade data: U.S. Trade Representative 2023; Taiwan Bureau of Foreign Trade; China Customs Statistics

Navigating Uncertainty: The Path Forward

Key Strategic Imperatives

1. Strengthen Deterrence (Green and Hicks 2024)

  • Military capability
  • Alliance coordination
  • Clear communication

2. Maintain Dialogue (Bush 2021)

  • Crisis management
  • Cultural/economic exchange
  • Track II diplomacy

3. Diversify Supply Chains (Kennedy and Sutter 2024)

  • CHIPS Act implementation
  • Friendshoring initiatives
  • Resilience over efficiency

4. Support Democratic Taiwan (Diamond 2024)

  • International space
  • Institutional ties
  • People-to-people exchange

Critical Questions for the Future

Near-term (2025-2027)

  • Can deterrence prevent conflict?
  • How will U.S. elections affect policy?
  • Will chip diversification succeed?
  • Can cross-strait dialogue resume?

Medium-term (2027-2035)

  • Will strategic ambiguity hold?
  • Can Taiwan maintain its tech edge?
  • How will demographics shift in Taiwan?
  • What role for multilateral frameworks?

The future depends on multiple interacting variables. High uncertainty around PRC intentions combines with high stakes for U.S. credibility and regional stability.

Conclusion: Stakes and Choices

The Stakes

  • Global economy depends on Taiwan chips
  • Democratic values tested by authoritarian pressure
  • U.S. credibility tied to Taiwan’s security
  • Regional order hangs in the balance

The Choice

Not whether to engage, but how:

  • Deterrence + dialogue
  • Clarity + flexibility
  • Partnership + prudence

Taiwan is not just a policy challenge—it’s a test of whether democracies can sustain partnerships, maintain technological leadership, and uphold international norms in an era of great power competition.
