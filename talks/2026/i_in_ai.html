Karl Ho

School of Economic, Political and Policy Sciences

Prepared for Office Hours After Dark, February 28, 2026

Thank you for having me. I want to start with a confession. I teach data science. I teach machine learning. I use AI every day — to code, to draft, to analyze, to think. And the more I use it, the more I realize how much of what I think I know about AI is actually marketing, metaphor, or momentum. So tonight I want to do something simple. I want to tell you what I actually know about AI, what I know I don’t know, and why the space between those two things is the most important territory we should be paying attention to.

Overview

A question in five parts:

  1. What is AI, actually? — A simple introduction
  2. How does AI help us think? — AI as scaffolding
  3. Where does AI fail, and why? — Structural failure modes
  4. What can be fixed and what can’t? — The boundary of improvement
  5. How do we grow with it? — Technology and the human arc

Here’s where we’re going. Five sections. I’m going to try to be honest about each one — not optimistic, not pessimistic, just honest. Some of this will be things you already know. Some of it, I hope, will surprise you. And some of it will be me saying “I don’t know” — which I think is the most underrated sentence in the AI conversation right now.

Part I: What Is AI, Actually?

Let’s start with the basics. Not because you don’t know anything about AI — you probably know quite a bit. But because I think the basics have been buried under so much hype that it’s worth digging them out again.

What I Tell My Students on Day One

The honest version

AI is pattern recognition, optimization, and prediction under uncertainty.

That’s it. Everything else is engineering and marketing.

  • A model sees data
  • It finds patterns in that data
  • It uses those patterns to make predictions about new data
  • It optimizes to reduce the gap between its predictions and reality

When I walk into my first class each semester, I strip it down to this. AI is pattern recognition, optimization, and prediction under uncertainty. That’s the foundation. Everything — GPT, image generators, self-driving cars, recommendation engines — everything is a variation on this theme. A model sees data. It finds patterns. It uses those patterns to guess what comes next. And it adjusts itself to guess better. That’s not magic. It’s not thinking. It’s math — very impressive math, at very impressive scale, but math.

What the Marketing Says Instead

What you hear:

  • “AI understands your question”
  • “AI reasons about the problem”
  • “AI is creative”
  • “AI learns like a human”
  • “AI knows the answer”

What’s happening:

  • Statistical pattern matching on tokens
  • Conditional probability estimation
  • Recombination of training data
  • Gradient descent on loss functions
  • Next-token prediction

Now here’s what the marketing says. “AI understands.” “AI reasons.” “AI is creative.” And I want to be very careful here — I’m not saying these systems aren’t impressive. They are staggeringly impressive. But “understands” is doing a lot of work in those sentences. What’s actually happening is statistical pattern matching. The system doesn’t understand your question. It predicts what tokens are most likely to follow the tokens you gave it. That’s a meaningful distinction — not because the output is bad, but because when the output goes wrong, you need to understand why it went wrong, and the marketing vocabulary gives you no tools to do that.

Three Things That Are True at Once

Holding complexity

  1. AI produces remarkable outputs. This is undeniable.
  2. AI does not understand what it produces. This is also undeniable.
  3. These two facts are not contradictory. This is the hard part.

The challenge is not choosing between enthusiasm and skepticism.

It is holding both simultaneously.

I think the public conversation about AI is broken because people think they have to choose sides. Either you’re excited about AI or you’re worried about it. But three things are true simultaneously. AI produces remarkable outputs. AI does not understand what it produces. And these two facts are not contradictory. A calculator doesn’t understand arithmetic. A thermostat doesn’t understand temperature. And GPT doesn’t understand language. But all three are useful — as long as you don’t confuse the output for comprehension. The challenge is holding both truths at the same time.

Part II: AI as Scaffolding

Now, if AI doesn’t understand, what is it doing when it helps us? I want to introduce a concept from learning theory that I think clarifies this better than any engineering framework.

Vygotsky’s Zone of Proximal Development

Lev Vygotsky (1896–1934):

  • The Zone of Proximal Development (ZPD) is the space between what a learner can do alone and what they can do with help
  • Scaffolding: temporary support that enables performance beyond current ability
  • The key word is temporary — scaffolding is meant to come down

Lev Vygotsky was a Soviet psychologist who, in the 1930s, developed the concept of the Zone of Proximal Development. The idea is simple: there’s a space between what you can do by yourself and what you can do with help. That space is where learning happens. And the help — the temporary support that lets you perform above your current level — is scaffolding. The critical word is temporary. Scaffolding is supposed to come down. You use it to build capacity, and then you remove it, because the person can now do the thing on their own.

When AI Is Good Scaffolding

AI as scaffolding works when it:

  • Helps you draft something you then revise with your own judgment
  • Automates the routine so you can focus on the conceptual
  • Reveals patterns in data you wouldn’t have seen manually
  • Gives you a starting structure you then populate with your knowledge
  • Makes the unfamiliar accessible — a bridge to a new domain

In each case, the human is doing the thinking. AI is doing the lifting.

When does AI work as good scaffolding? When it handles the mechanical so you can focus on the conceptual. When it gives you a rough draft you then revise. When it surfaces patterns in data that would have taken you weeks to find. When it helps you enter a new domain by translating jargon into plain language. In all of these cases, notice: the human is still doing the thinking. AI is doing the lifting. That’s scaffolding.

When AI Becomes a Crutch

AI becomes a crutch when it:

  • Produces the final output and you accept it without revision
  • Replaces practice — the cognitive exercise that builds skill
  • Creates an illusion of understanding in the user
  • Makes the user less capable over time, not more
  • Removes the productive struggle where learning actually happens

Ted Chiang (2024): “Using ChatGPT to complete assignments is like bringing a forklift into the weight room.”

But here’s where it goes wrong. When AI produces the final output and you just accept it, that’s not scaffolding — that’s replacement. When you stop practicing a skill because AI does it for you, that’s not support — that’s atrophy. The science fiction writer Ted Chiang put it perfectly: using ChatGPT to complete assignments is like bringing a forklift into the weight room. You’ll never build cognitive fitness that way. And the MIT Media Lab has now confirmed this empirically. Kosmyna and colleagues used EEG to measure brain activity during essay writing. Students using ChatGPT showed the weakest neural connectivity. They also couldn’t remember what they’d written minutes after writing it. The scaffolding wasn’t coming down. It was becoming the building.

The Scaffolding Test

Ask yourself:

After using AI for a task, can you now do that task better without AI than before?

  • Yes → AI was scaffolding. The support built capacity.
  • No → AI was a crutch. The support replaced capacity.

This is not a question about AI. It is a question about you.

So here’s the test I give my students, and I apply it to myself. After using AI for a task, can you now do that task better without AI than you could before? If yes, AI was scaffolding — it built your capacity. If no, AI was a crutch — it replaced your capacity. And notice: this is not a question about the technology. It’s a question about the user. The same tool can be scaffolding for one person and a crutch for another, depending on how deliberately they use it. The “I” in AI is the variable that matters most.

Part III: Where AI Fails, and Why

Now let’s talk about failures. Not to be negative — but because I think the why behind AI failures is the least understood and most important part of the conversation.

The Error Is Always There

A foundational principle of machine learning:

Every model is wrong. The question is whether it is useful. (George Box, 1976)

  • No model achieves zero error
  • Better models don’t eliminate error — they move it
  • Errors have migrated from visible (six-fingered hands) to invisible (plausible but wrong reasoning)

Let me start with a principle I teach on day one of every machine learning course. Every model is wrong. This is not a criticism. It’s a mathematical fact. George Box said it in 1976 and it hasn’t stopped being true. The question is never whether a model has errors but whether it’s useful despite them. Now here’s what’s happened as AI has improved: the errors haven’t disappeared. They’ve migrated. Two years ago, AI-generated images had six fingers. That was easy to catch. Today, the images look perfect. The errors have moved from the surface to the interior — from the visible to the plausible. The model got better at looking right. It did not get better at being right.

Failure Mode 1: Hallucination

What it is: AI generates confident, fluent, false outputs

Why it happens:

  • LLMs are next-token prediction machines
  • They optimize for plausibility, not truth
  • There is no internal representation of “correctness”
  • Confidence and accuracy are decoupled — the system has no way to know what it doesn’t know

Warning

Hallucination is not a bug. It is a consequence of the architecture.

Hallucination. The word itself is misleading — it implies the machine is seeing something that isn’t there, like a person with a fever. What’s actually happening is simpler and more troubling. LLMs predict the next most likely token. They optimize for plausibility — for output that sounds right. But they have no internal representation of truth, no way to check their output against reality. Confidence and accuracy are completely decoupled. The system speaks with the same fluency whether it’s right or wrong. This is not a bug to be patched. It’s a consequence of the architecture. You build a system to predict plausible sequences, and it produces plausible sequences — including plausible falsehoods.

Failure Mode 2: Distributional Shift

What it is: Model performance degrades when real-world data differs from training data

Why it happens:

  • Models learn the statistical properties of their training set
  • The world changes; the training set doesn’t
  • Novel situations have no pattern to match

Example: A model trained on pre-pandemic economic data cannot predict pandemic-era behavior — not because it’s poorly trained, but because the world it learned no longer exists.

Second failure mode: distributional shift. This one is intuitive once you see it. A model learns patterns from its training data. If the real world changes — and the real world always changes — the patterns no longer apply. A model trained on pre-pandemic economic data can’t predict pandemic-era behavior. Not because the model is bad, but because the world it learned no longer exists. This is a fundamental limitation of any system that learns from historical data. It can only predict futures that resemble the past. When the future doesn’t cooperate, the model breaks — often silently, with no warning.

Failure Mode 3: No Causal Reasoning

What it is: AI identifies correlations but cannot reason about causes

What AI can do:

  • “X and Y tend to co-occur”
  • “When X increases, Y increases”
  • “In the training data, X predicted Y”

What AI cannot do:

  • “X causes Y”
  • “Intervening on X will change Y”
  • “The mechanism connecting X and Y is…”

Judea Pearl (2018): “Current machine learning systems operate in a purely associational mode.”

Third: the absence of causal reasoning. This is, I think, the most consequential limitation and the least discussed in public. AI systems are extraordinarily good at finding correlations. They can tell you that X and Y tend to co-occur, that when X goes up Y goes up. But they cannot tell you that X causes Y. They cannot tell you what would happen if you intervened on X. They cannot describe the mechanism. Judea Pearl — one of the most important figures in the study of causation — has been saying this for years: current ML systems operate in a purely associational mode. This matters because most of the decisions we care about — in policy, medicine, law, education — are causal questions. “If we do this, what happens?” AI can’t answer that question. It can only tell you what happened before.

Failure Mode 4: No Self-Monitoring

What it is: AI systems cannot evaluate the quality of their own outputs

  • No mechanism for doubt
  • No capacity to flag uncertainty
  • Output confidence is uniform — the system speaks the same way when it knows and when it doesn’t

A machine without a mirror

The system cannot know what it doesn’t know. It has no model of its own competence boundaries.

And fourth: no self-monitoring. This is what I’ve been calling “a machine without a mirror.” AI systems have no mechanism for doubt. They cannot evaluate the quality of their own output. They produce confident text whether they’re drawing on robust patterns or fabricating entirely. There’s no internal signal that says “I’m uncertain about this” or “this is outside my competence.” The system has no model of its own boundaries. This means the burden of quality assessment falls entirely on the user. Every time. For every output. Which brings us to the economics of verification — but I’ll save that for another talk.

Why These Failures Are Structural

The pattern:

These are not failures of scale, data, or training.

They are consequences of how the systems are built.

Failure Root cause More data helps?
Hallucination Optimizes plausibility, not truth ❌
Distributional shift Training ≠ deployment conditions ❌
No causal reasoning Associational architecture ❌
No self-monitoring No internal model of competence ❌

Now here’s the point I want to land on. None of these failures are about insufficient data or inadequate training. They are structural. They arise from the architecture itself. Hallucination happens because the system optimizes for plausibility, not truth. Distributional shift happens because training data is always historical. The absence of causal reasoning happens because the architecture is associational. The absence of self-monitoring happens because there is no internal model of competence. More data doesn’t fix any of these. Bigger models don’t fix any of these. These are design consequences, not engineering shortcomings.

Part IV: What Can Be Improved and What Can’t

So now we arrive at the section where I have to be most honest. Because some of these problems are being worked on, and some progress is real. But some problems may resist solution — not because we haven’t tried hard enough, but because they may require a fundamentally different kind of system.

What I Think Can Be Improved

Engineering problems with plausible solutions:

  • Retrieval-Augmented Generation (RAG): Ground outputs in retrieved documents — reduces (doesn’t eliminate) hallucination
  • Tool use and function calling: Let models delegate to reliable systems (calculators, databases, APIs)
  • Better evaluation frameworks: Systematic benchmarking of failure modes
  • Fine-tuning on domain data: Improve performance in specific contexts
  • Human-in-the-loop workflows: Design systems that surface uncertainty to human reviewers

Let me start with what I think can be improved. Retrieval-augmented generation — having the model look things up rather than relying solely on its parameters — genuinely helps with hallucination. Tool use — letting the model call a calculator or query a database — helps with accuracy on tasks where the tool is reliable. Better evaluation frameworks, domain-specific fine-tuning, human-in-the-loop workflows — these are all real engineering advances. They make the systems more reliable in practice. I’m not dismissing them.

The Verification Ladder

  • Low stakes: brainstorm, rephrase, outline (no-check is fine)
  • Medium stakes: plausibility check + source check
  • High stakes: cross-check + ground truth test
  • Rule: higher impact → higher verification

AI makes producing text cheap; it makes trust expensive. So I use a ladder.

For low-stakes work—brainstorming, tone, first drafts—I don’t overthink it. For medium stakes—anything customer-facing, policy, numbers—I do quick plausibility and check sources. For high stakes—legal, financial, medical, production code, big decisions—I treat AI like a junior assistant: I cross-check with another system or a human, and when possible I test against ground truth.

The point isn’t paranoia—it’s matching verification to consequences.

What I’m Not Sure Can Be Improved

Problems that may require architectural change:

  • Genuine uncertainty quantification: The system knowing — and communicating — how confident it should be
  • Causal reasoning: Moving from “what correlates” to “what causes”
  • Epistemic self-awareness: The system modeling its own knowledge boundaries
  • Robustness to distribution shift: Handling situations genuinely unlike anything in training

These may not be engineering problems. They may be architecture problems.

But here’s where my confidence drops — and I think intellectual honesty requires saying so. I’m not sure current architectures can achieve genuine uncertainty quantification — the system knowing how confident it should be, not just how confident it sounds. I’m not sure associational systems can be made to reason causally. I’m not sure a next-token predictor can model its own knowledge boundaries. And I’m not sure any system trained on historical data can robustly handle situations genuinely unlike its training. These may not be engineering problems at all. They may require fundamentally different architectures — systems we haven’t built yet, that work on different principles. I could be wrong about this. But I think saying “I don’t know” is more honest than pretending the current trajectory will inevitably solve them.

Scaled Errors: Paper Cuts Become Risk

  • Harm scales with: Error rate × Volume × Authority × Time-to-detect
  • LLM failure mode: confident + plausible + slightly wrong
  • Workplace reality: small errors repeat and normalize

One mistake in one email is a paper cut. But if a tool produces 200 emails a day, those paper cuts add up.

And the more “official” the output feels—because it’s well-written—the more authority it gets. The scary part isn’t dramatic failure; it’s subtle drift: tiny factual errors, slightly wrong summaries, policy edge cases omitted, numbers that look right but aren’t.

Integrated intelligence is about lowering time-to-detect and reducing “authority without proof.”

The Honest Map

What I know:

  • How these systems work mechanically
  • Where the error comes from structurally
  • What engineering solutions exist today
  • That the outputs are often remarkable
  • That the failures are often invisible

What I don’t know:

  • Whether current architectures have fundamental ceilings
  • Whether hallucination is eliminable or only reducible
  • How to build systems that genuinely know what they don’t know
  • Whether the next paradigm will emerge from scaling or from rethinking
  • What we’re losing cognitively that we can’t yet measure

So here’s my honest map. On the left: what I know. How these systems work mechanically. Where the errors come from. What engineering mitigations exist. That the outputs are remarkable and the failures are often invisible. On the right: what I don’t know. Whether there are fundamental ceilings. Whether hallucination can be eliminated or only managed. How to build genuinely self-aware systems. Whether the next breakthrough will come from scaling or from a completely different approach. And — maybe most importantly — what we’re losing cognitively that we don’t yet have the tools to measure. I think anyone who tells you they have confident answers to the questions on the right is either selling something or not paying close enough attention.

Part V: How We Grow with Technology

So where does this leave us? Not in despair — because the history of technology and human cognition is not a story of decline. It’s a story of change. And change, when we’re deliberate about it, can be growth.

We’ve Been Here Before

Each cognitive technology changed what humans could do — and what they stopped doing:

Technology Gained Lost
Writing (3200 BCE) External memory, legal systems, literature Oral memory traditions, bardic culture
Printing (1440) Mass literacy, scientific communication Scribal arts, controlled knowledge access
Calculator (1970s) Speed, accuracy in computation Mental arithmetic fluency
Search engines (1990s) Instant information access Deep recall, research patience
AI (2020s) Drafting, pattern recognition, automation ?

We’ve been here before. Every major cognitive technology changed both what humans could do and what they stopped doing. Writing gave us external memory and literature. It also ended the oral memory traditions that had sustained human culture for millennia. Socrates warned about this — he thought writing would make us forgetful. He was right! And he was wrong, because what we gained was worth what we lost. Printing gave us mass literacy and scientific communication. It also ended the era of controlled knowledge. Calculators gave us speed. They took away mental arithmetic. Search engines gave us instant access to information. They took away the deep recall and research patience that Nicholas Carr mourned in 2008. Now AI. The gains are clear. The losses — the question mark — are what we’re here to think about tonight.

Carr → Chiang → Now

A lineage of concern:

  • Nicholas Carr (2008): “I’m not thinking the way I used to think.” — The Shallows: Internet shifts us from meditative to utilitarian intelligence

  • Ted Chiang (2024): “Your job is not to turn in completed assignments; it’s to learn how to think.” — AI bypasses the cognitive exercise that builds capacity

  • Kosmyna et al. (2025): EEG evidence that ChatGPT users show weakest neural connectivity — cognitive debt is measurable

The concern has moved from distraction (Carr) to delegation (Chiang) to measurable atrophy (Kosmyna).

I want to trace a lineage here because I think it matters. In 2008, Nicholas Carr wrote “Is Google Making Us Stupid?” — and his concern was about distraction. The internet was scattering our attention, making deep reading difficult. In 2024, Ted Chiang extended the argument to delegation. AI doesn’t just distract us — it does the thinking for us. And in 2025, Kosmyna and colleagues at MIT provided the neuroscience: EEG scans showing that people who wrote with ChatGPT had measurably weaker brain connectivity than those who wrote alone. The concern has escalated from distraction to delegation to measurable cognitive atrophy. Each step is more serious than the last.

Growing With vs. Growing Around

Growing with AI

  • Use AI to enter new domains, then learn to navigate them yourself
  • Treat AI outputs as first drafts requiring human judgment
  • Maintain practices that exercise independent reasoning
  • Design workflows that surface uncertainty rather than hide it
  • Ask: “What did I learn from this interaction?”

Growing around AI

  • Depend on AI for tasks you used to do yourself
  • Accept outputs without verification or revision
  • Lose skills gradually, invisibly
  • Confuse AI fluency for your own understanding
  • Ask: “How do I get the output I need?”

So the question becomes: do we grow with AI or grow around it? Growing with AI means using it to enter new domains and then building your own capacity. Treating outputs as first drafts. Maintaining independent reasoning practices. Designing workflows that surface uncertainty rather than hide it. The test question: “What did I learn from this interaction?” Growing around AI means depending on it for tasks you used to do yourself. Accepting outputs. Losing skills gradually. Confusing the machine’s fluency for your own understanding. The test question: “How do I get the output I need?” These are fundamentally different relationships. One builds the I in AI. The other hollows it out.

Principles for Integration

What I try to practice (not always successfully):

  1. Separate AI time from thinking time. Don’t let the tool colonize every cognitive moment.

  2. Verify structurally, not just occasionally. Build verification into the workflow, not the afterthought.

  3. Maintain skills you’ve delegated. The muscle metaphor is real.

  4. Demand uncertainty from your tools. If the system can’t tell you how confident it is, that’s information about the system.

  5. Teach with scaffolding in mind. The goal is to build the student’s capacity, not the tool’s indispensability.

Here are the principles I try to practice. I don’t always succeed. First: separate AI time from thinking time. Not every cognitive task should start with a prompt. Second: build verification into the workflow, not as an afterthought. Third: maintain the skills you’ve delegated. If you stop doing something, you stop being able to do it — that’s not philosophy, that’s neuroscience. Fourth: demand uncertainty from your tools. If the system can’t tell you how confident it is, treat that as a red flag, not a feature. Fifth — and this one matters most to me as a teacher: design AI use as scaffolding that builds the student’s capacity, not as a crutch that builds the tool’s indispensability.

Integrated Intelligence: Design the Human + AI System

  • Don’t optimize for generation — optimize for judgment
  • Add structure: constraints, sources, logs, review budgets
  • Make learning real: Request → Run → Review → Record

“Human-in-the-loop” is not a strategy if the human has to review everything forever.

Integrated intelligence means we design the system: what the AI is allowed to do, what it must cite, what gets logged, what gets reviewed, and how we learn from failures.

My simple loop is Request, Run, Review, Record. Record is the missing step: we don’t just fix today’s output; we capture the failure mode so tomorrow’s work is safer and less tiring.

Where This Leaves Us

The “I” in AI is not the artificial.

It is the intelligence that decides what to delegate, what to verify,

what to learn, and what to think through alone.


The question is not whether AI changes how we think.

The question is whether we are deliberate enough

to grow with it rather than merely depend on it.

I’ll close with this. The “I” in AI is not the artificial part. It’s you. It’s the intelligence that decides what to delegate, what to verify, what to learn, and what to think through alone. The technology will keep improving. The marketing will keep overselling. The failures will keep migrating from visible to invisible. But the “I” — the human judgment at the center of the system — that’s the variable we control. The question is not whether AI changes how we think. It changes how we think. The question is whether we’re deliberate enough to grow with it rather than merely depend on it. That’s what I know. And that’s what I’m still trying to figure out. Thank you.
