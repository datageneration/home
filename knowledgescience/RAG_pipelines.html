Connection to Knowledge Mining

RAG is the bridge between the text mining methods we have studied (quanteda, Wordfish, STM) and the LLM/agentic systems covered in the previous lecture. It enables LLMs to work with your data.

Why RAG Matters for Research

Core insight: RAG does not replace the LLM — it augments it with relevant, verifiable external knowledge at query time, without retraining.

Part I: The Retrieval Problem

Why LLMs Need External Knowledge

LLMs face fundamental limitations for research applications:

  • Knowledge cutoff: Training data has a fixed end date — the model cannot know about events, publications, or data collected after training
  • No access to proprietary data: Your survey data, interview transcripts, government reports, and institutional documents are not in the training corpus
  • Hallucination: LLMs generate plausible but fabricated citations, statistics, and claims — dangerous for research
  • No source attribution: Standard LLMs cannot point to specific passages or documents that support their claims

For Researchers

If you ask an LLM to summarize the latest DOD China Military Power Report or analyze your TEDS 2016 survey data, it cannot do so accurately without RAG — it will either refuse or hallucinate.

Three Approaches to Grounding LLMs

Approach Method Cost Flexibility
Fine-tuning Retrain model on domain data Very high Low (static)
In-context learning Paste documents into prompt Low Limited by context window
RAG Retrieve relevant chunks at query time Moderate High (dynamic)

RAG combines the best of both worlds: it is cheaper than fine-tuning and more scalable than in-context learning, while providing source attribution and access to continuously updated knowledge.

The RAG Intuition

Think of RAG as giving the LLM access to a research library at the moment it needs to answer a question:

  1. Student asks a question → the user submits a query
  2. Librarian finds relevant books → the retriever searches the knowledge base
  3. Student reads the relevant passages → retrieved chunks are injected into the prompt
  4. Student writes an informed answer → the LLM generates a response grounded in the retrieved context

The key difference from a web search: RAG operates over your curated knowledge base — your PDFs, your datasets, your institutional documents — not the open web.

Part II: Traditional RAG Architecture

The RAG Pipeline

Step 1: Document Ingestion and Chunking

Raw documents must be split into manageable passages before embedding:

Chunking strategies:

Strategy Method Best For
Fixed-size Split every N tokens with overlap Simple, predictable
Sentence-based Split on sentence boundaries Preserving grammatical units
Paragraph-based Split on paragraph breaks Structured documents
Semantic Split where topic shifts (using embeddings) Research papers, reports
Recursive Try large chunks first, subdivide if too long General-purpose

Critical Design Decision

Chunk size directly affects retrieval quality. Too small → fragments lose context. Too large → dilutes relevance and wastes context window. Typical range: 256–1024 tokens with 50–100 token overlap.

Step 2: Embeddings

Text chunks are converted to dense numerical vectors that capture semantic meaning:

\[\text{embed}: \text{text} \rightarrow \mathbb{R}^d\]

where \(d\) is the embedding dimension (typically 768–3072) and semantically similar texts are mapped to nearby points in vector space.

Similarity between two texts is measured by cosine similarity:

\[\text{sim}(\mathbf{a}, \mathbf{b}) = \frac{\mathbf{a} \cdot \mathbf{b}}{||\mathbf{a}|| \cdot ||\mathbf{b}||} = \frac{\sum_{i=1}^{d} a_i b_i}{\sqrt{\sum_{i=1}^{d} a_i^2} \cdot \sqrt{\sum_{i=1}^{d} b_i^2}}\]

This measures the angle between two vectors, ranging from -1 (opposite) to 1 (identical), regardless of vector magnitude. Texts about similar topics cluster together even when they use different words.

Step 3: Vector Databases

Vector databases store embeddings and enable fast approximate nearest-neighbor (ANN) search:

Database Type Strengths
Chroma Open-source, in-process Easy prototyping, Python-native
Qdrant Open-source, Rust Fast, hybrid search, self-hostable
Pinecone Managed cloud Zero-ops, serverless scaling
pgvector PostgreSQL extension Combines with relational queries
FAISS Facebook library Research-grade, GPU-accelerated

Step 4: Retrieval and Generation

At query time, the system retrieves relevant chunks and injects them into the LLM prompt:

System: You are a research assistant. Answer the user's question 
based ONLY on the following context. If the context does not contain
the answer, say "I don't have enough information."

Context:
[Chunk 1]: "The 2024 DOD report states that China's nuclear 
warhead stockpile exceeded 600 operational warheads..."
[Chunk 2]: "Beijing continues to modernize its ICBM force with 
silo-based and road-mobile DF-41 deployments..."
[Chunk 3]: "The PLA Navy commissioned its third aircraft carrier,
the Fujian, for operational deployment in 2024..."

User: What is the current state of China's nuclear modernization?

The LLM generates a response grounded in the retrieved context, with the ability to cite specific chunks as sources.

A re-ranker is a cross-encoder model that scores each (query, document) pair for fine-grained relevance:

Why two stages? The initial retrieval (bi-encoder) is fast but approximate. The re-ranker (cross-encoder) is slow but accurate. Combining them gives speed and precision — retrieve broadly, then rank precisely.

Part III: From Traditional to Agentic RAG

Limitations of Traditional RAG

Traditional RAG follows a fixed pipeline: retrieve once, generate once, stop.

This fails when:

  • The query is ambiguous — the system cannot ask clarifying questions
  • The answer requires multiple sources — a single retrieval pass may miss relevant documents across databases
  • The query requires multi-step reasoning — “compare X across Y reports” needs iterative retrieval
  • Retrieved context is insufficient or conflicting — the system has no mechanism to detect and recover
  • The knowledge base is heterogeneous — mixing PDFs, databases, APIs, and web sources

Research Example

“How has the framing of ‘technology transfer’ evolved across DOD China reports from 2005–2025?” requires searching across 20+ documents, identifying relevant passages in each, tracking semantic shifts over time, and synthesizing findings — far beyond a single retrieve-and-generate cycle.

Traditional RAG vs. Agentic RAG

Dimension Traditional RAG Agentic RAG
Retrieval Single query, single source Iterative, multi-source
Query handling Fixed pipeline Agent decomposes and refines
Decision-making None Agent selects tools and sources
Error recovery None Agent detects failures, retries
Memory Stateless Maintains session context
Tool use Retrieval only Search + SQL + APIs + code execution
Output Single response Iteratively refined answer

Traditional RAG is a lookup tool. Agentic RAG is a research assistant.

Agentic RAG Architecture

The Four Agent Roles

Agentic RAG decomposes the retrieval process across specialized agents:

1. Router Agent — directs the query to the appropriate data source. A question about survey demographics goes to the SQL database; a question about policy language goes to the vector store; a question about current events goes to a web API.

2. Query Planner — decomposes complex questions into sub-queries. “Compare nuclear vs. conventional force modernization across 2015 and 2025 reports” becomes four targeted retrievals.

3. Retrieval Agent — executes the search, evaluates results, and reformulates queries if initial retrieval is insufficient. If the first search returns low-relevance chunks, it tries alternative phrasings or broader queries.

4. Evaluator Agent — assesses whether the retrieved context is sufficient to answer the question. If not, it triggers additional retrieval rounds. If context is contradictory, it flags the conflict.

Graph RAG: Knowledge Graphs + RAG

Graph RAG augments vector retrieval with structured knowledge graphs:

Knowledge graphs capture relationships (entity → relation → entity) that vector similarity cannot. For political science research: “Xi Jinping” → “commands” → “PLA Rocket Force” → “deploys” → “DF-41 ICBMs” — this chain of relations enables multi-hop reasoning that pure vector search misses.

RAG Architecture Landscape (2026)

Architecture Complexity Best For
Naive RAG Low Simple Q&A over single document
Hybrid RAG Medium Production baseline — dense + sparse search
Self-RAG Medium Model decides when to retrieve
Corrective RAG Medium Validates retrieval relevance before generation
Graph RAG High Multi-hop reasoning over connected entities
Agentic RAG High Complex, multi-source research workflows
Agentic Graph RAG Very High Autonomous exploration of knowledge networks

Practical Advice

Start with Hybrid RAG. Move to Agentic RAG only when your queries require multi-step reasoning, multiple data sources, or iterative refinement. Complexity has costs — more LLM calls, higher latency, harder debugging.

Part IV: RAG for Research

Research Applications

RAG transforms several common research workflows:

Literature review: Build a vector database from thousands of papers. Query with research questions. Get synthesized answers with citations to specific passages.

Policy document analysis: Index government reports (DOD, UN, WHO). Track how specific concepts evolve across years of reporting. Compare framing across agencies or countries.

Qualitative data analysis: Index interview transcripts or focus group data. Query for themes, contradictions, or specific respondent perspectives. Ground findings in specific quotes.

Survey data augmentation: Combine structured survey data (SQL) with unstructured policy documents (vector DB) via Agentic RAG to contextualize quantitative findings.

Evaluation: Is RAG Working?

RAG systems require evaluation at two stages:

Retrieval quality — Are the right chunks being retrieved?

  • Recall@k: What fraction of relevant documents appear in the top-k results?
  • Precision@k: What fraction of retrieved documents are actually relevant?
  • MRR (Mean Reciprocal Rank): How high does the first relevant result rank?

Generation quality — Is the LLM using the context correctly?

  • Faithfulness: Does the answer accurately reflect the retrieved context?
  • Answer relevance: Does the answer address the user’s question?
  • Groundedness: Can every claim be traced to a retrieved source?

Frameworks: RAGAS, LlamaIndex Evaluation, Trulens provide automated metrics for RAG evaluation.

Common Failure Modes

Understanding how RAG fails is essential for building robust research systems:

Failure Mode Cause Mitigation
Missing retrieval Relevant chunks not indexed or poorly chunked Improve chunking, add metadata filters
Low relevance Embedding model does not capture domain semantics Use domain-specific embeddings or hybrid search
Context overflow Too many chunks exceed LLM context window Re-rank and select top-k, use longer-context models
Hallucinated synthesis LLM ignores or misrepresents retrieved context Use structured prompts, require citations
Stale data Vector index not updated when source documents change Automate re-indexing pipelines
Cross-document confusion LLM conflates information from different sources Include metadata (source, date) in prompt

Part V: Ethical and Practical Considerations

Data Governance and Privacy

RAG introduces specific research ethics considerations:

  • Data residency: If using cloud embedding APIs or managed vector databases, your research data leaves your network. Sensitive data (interview transcripts, protected records) may require self-hosted solutions
  • Embedding irreversibility: Embeddings cannot be decoded back to original text — but proximity attacks can reveal content. Embeddings of sensitive data should be treated as sensitive
  • Attribution and provenance: RAG enables citation of sources, but the system must be designed to preserve and surface provenance metadata
  • Reproducibility: RAG outputs depend on the state of the vector index at query time. Version your indexes alongside your code and data

Summary

  1. RAG grounds LLMs in external, verifiable knowledge — essential for research integrity
  2. Hybrid search (dense + sparse) is the 2026 production baseline at 91% recall
  3. Agentic RAG transforms retrieval from a static lookup into an iterative, intelligent research process
  4. Start simple (Hybrid RAG), add complexity (agents) only when your research questions demand it
