Part I: Caveats of AI

What AI Actually Is (and Isn’t)

AI IS:

  • Pattern recognition at scale
  • Statistical prediction machines
  • Tools that process and generate text
  • Trained on human-created content
  • Probabilistic, not deterministic

AI IS NOT:

  • Conscious or sentient
  • A replacement for human judgment
  • Always accurate or truthful
  • A source of original knowledge
  • Understanding meaning (just patterns)

Key Insight

LLMs predict the next most likely token based on training data. They don’t “know” anything—they pattern-match.

The Hallucination Problem

Hallucination: AI generates confident, plausible-sounding but factually incorrect information.

Why It Happens:

  • LLMs optimize for fluency, not accuracy
  • Training data contains errors and contradictions
  • Model has no grounding in reality
  • Confidence ≠ Correctness

Examples:

  • Fabricated citations and references
  • Invented statistics and dates
  • Non-existent people or events
  • Plausible but wrong explanations

Real Cases

  • Lawyers cited fake cases generated by ChatGPT (2023)
  • Academic papers retracted for AI-generated fake references
  • News articles with fabricated quotes

Bias in AI Systems

Sources of Bias:

  1. Training Data Bias
    • Overrepresentation of certain viewpoints
    • Western, English-language dominance
    • Historical biases encoded in text
  2. Algorithmic Bias
    • Optimization objectives
    • Feedback loops
    • Evaluation metrics

Manifestations:

  • Gender stereotypes in outputs
  • Cultural assumptions
  • Political leanings
  • Socioeconomic blind spots

Warning

Research on LLM bias shows systematic patterns in how models describe different demographic groups.

The “Stochastic Parrot” Critique

“LLMs are stochastic parrots—they learn to produce plausible-sounding text without understanding meaning.” — Bender et al. (2021)

Implications for Research:

  • AI cannot truly understand your research question
  • Cannot reason about novel situations outside training
  • May reproduce errors from training data
  • Lacks the ability to verify its own outputs

Your Role: You remain the expert. AI is a tool, not a colleague.

Part II: Safe and Smart Use of AI

A Framework for AI Use

When To Use AI

Good Use Cases:

  • Brainstorming ideas and approaches
  • Drafting initial outlines
  • Explaining complex concepts
  • Debugging code errors
  • Summarizing long documents
  • Reformatting text or data
  • Translation (with verification)
  • Learning new techniques

Workflow Enhancement:

When NOT to Use AI

Avoid AI For:

  • Generating citations or references
  • Creating data or statistics
  • Final drafts without review
  • Domain-specific facts without verification
  • Ethical or legal advice
  • Anything requiring current information

Red Flags:

Stop If:

  • AI provides specific statistics
  • AI cites specific papers
  • AI claims certainty about facts
  • Output seems “too good to be true”
  • You can’t verify the claims

The Verification Imperative

Every AI output requires verification:

Output Type Verification Method
Facts/Statistics Check primary sources
Citations Look up in Google Scholar/library
Code Run and test thoroughly
Explanations Cross-reference with textbooks
Data analysis Verify with manual spot-checks

Rule of Thumb

If you can’t verify it, don’t use it.

Prompt Engineering Best Practices

Structure Your Prompts:

**Context**: I am a graduate student working on [topic]

**Task**: Help me [specific request]

**Constraints**: 
- Do not make up citations
- If uncertain, say so
- Focus on [specific aspect]

**Format**: Provide output as [bullet points/code/etc.]

Key Principles:

  1. Be specific about what you need
  2. Provide relevant context
  3. Set explicit constraints
  4. Request uncertainty acknowledgment
  5. Iterate and refine

Context Engineering for Research

Building Persistent Context:

Create a PROJECT_CONTEXT.md file:

# Research Project Context

## Topic
[Your research question]

## Methodology
[Your approach]

## Key Terms
[Domain-specific definitions]

## Constraints
- No fabricated citations
- Acknowledge uncertainty
- Focus on methodology

Benefits:

  • Consistent AI behavior across sessions
  • Reduced need to repeat context
  • Better quality outputs
  • Documented AI interaction patterns

Note

This approach aligns with the “GEMINI.md” method discussed in industry practice.

AI-Assisted Coding: Ten Simple Rules

Based on Poldrack et al. (2025):

  1. Maintain domain expertise — AI can’t replace your knowledge
  2. Treat AI as a junior developer — Review everything
  3. Use incremental development — Small chunks, frequent testing
  4. Verify all outputs — Test, test, test
  5. Document AI interactions — Track what AI contributed
  1. Understand before accepting — Never use code you don’t understand
  2. Maintain coding skills — Practice without AI regularly
  3. Use appropriate tools — Match tool to task
  4. Consider reproducibility — Document AI tool versions
  5. Stay current — AI capabilities change rapidly

Part III: AI and Originality

What Is Originality?

Traditional View:

  • Novel ideas from the human mind
  • Synthesis of existing knowledge
  • Creative expression
  • Unique perspective and voice

With AI:

  • The question becomes more nuanced
  • Process matters as much as product
  • Human judgment remains central

Originality Spectrum:

Academic Integrity in the AI Era

The Core Principle Remains:

Your work should represent your understanding, your analysis, and your conclusions.

Acceptable:

  • Using AI to brainstorm
  • Getting explanations of concepts
  • Debugging your code
  • Improving clarity of your writing
  • Learning new techniques

Not Acceptable:

  • Submitting AI output as your own
  • Using AI to generate data
  • Having AI write your analysis
  • Not disclosing AI use
  • Using AI on prohibited assignments

The Transparency Requirement

Our Course Policy Requires:

When using AI tools, document:

  1. Full exchange — The complete conversation
  2. Tool specification — Which AI, which version
  3. Usage explanation — How you used it
  4. Rationale — Why you chose to use AI

Why This Matters

  • Transparency enables learning assessment
  • Helps you develop metacognitive skills
  • Builds good professional habits
  • Maintains academic integrity

Developing Your Own Voice

AI Can Help You Find Your Voice, Not Replace It:

  1. Use AI for feedback, not generation
  2. Compare AI suggestions to your instincts
  3. Practice writing without AI regularly
  4. Develop domain expertise that AI lacks
  5. Cultivate critical thinking about AI outputs

The Goal:

Become a researcher who uses AI tools skillfully, not one who depends on them.

Exercise

Try writing a paragraph without AI, then ask AI for feedback. Notice the difference between AI-generated text and AI-improved text.

Building Good Habits

Daily Practices:

  1. Start with your own thinking
    • Draft before consulting AI
    • Form hypotheses first
  2. Use AI as a dialogue partner
    • Ask questions, don’t just request output
    • Challenge AI responses
  3. Maintain verification habits
    • Always check facts
    • Test all code
  1. Document as you go
    • Keep your AI collaboration log
    • Note what works and what doesn’t
  2. Reflect regularly
    • Are you learning or just producing?
    • Is your understanding growing?
    • Can you do this without AI?
