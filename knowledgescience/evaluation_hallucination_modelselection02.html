Evaluating AI Systems

Evaluation Metrics, Hallucination Detection, and Model Selection

Karl Ho

Why Evaluation Matters

Large language models now write policy briefs, code legislative analyses, and summarize scholarly literature. But:

  • How do we know a model is correct?
  • How do we know it isn’t making things up?
  • How do we choose among dozens of competing models?

Evaluation is not an afterthought — it is the foundation of responsible deployment.

For political and social science applications, the cost of silent error is high: misattributed quotes, fabricated citations, biased coding of survey responses, spurious causal claims.

The Three Questions of AI Evaluation

Each question demands a different methodology, different tooling, and a different research design.

Part I — Evaluation Metrics

Evaluation is a Measurement Problem

We cannot improve what we cannot measure. But language is not a quantity:

  • There is rarely a single “correct” answer.
  • Two different outputs can both be correct, or both wrong.
  • Human judgments disagree even among trained annotators.

So evaluation in NLP has evolved through four generations:

  1. Reference-based n-gram overlap (BLEU, ROUGE)
  2. Embedding-based similarity (BERTScore)
  3. Benchmark suites (MMLU, HELM, BIG-bench)
  4. LLM-as-judge and human preference (Chatbot Arena, MT-Bench)

Generation 1: N-gram Overlap Metrics

BLEU (Bilingual Evaluation Understudy)

Developed in 2002 for machine translation. Measures precision of n-grams in the candidate against reference(s).

\[ \text{BLEU} = \underbrace{\text{BP}}_{\substack{\text{brevity} \\ \text{penalty}}} \cdot \exp\left( \underbrace{\sum_{n=1}^{N} w_n \log p_n}_{\substack{\text{weighted log} \\ \text{n-gram precision}}} \right) \]

where \(p_n\) is the fraction of candidate n-grams that appear in any reference, and BP penalizes outputs shorter than the reference to prevent gaming.

BLEU: Strengths and Limits

Strengths

  • Fast, deterministic, widely reported.
  • Correlates with human judgment at the corpus level.

Limits

  • Poor correlation at the sentence level.
  • Blind to meaning: “The vote passed” and “The measure carried” share no n-grams.
  • Penalizes legitimate paraphrase.
  • Useless for open-ended generation.

Warning

Do not use BLEU to compare two similarly-capable LLMs on the same task. The metric is too coarse.

ROUGE: Recall Where BLEU is Precision

ROUGE (Recall-Oriented Understudy for Gisting Evaluation) is the standard metric for summarization.

  • ROUGE-N: recall of n-grams
  • ROUGE-L: longest common subsequence
  • ROUGE-W: weighted LCS favoring contiguous matches

\[ \text{ROUGE-N} = \underbrace{\frac{\sum_{S \in \text{Ref}} \sum_{\text{n-gram} \in S} \text{Count}_{\text{match}}(\text{n-gram})}{\sum_{S \in \text{Ref}} \sum_{\text{n-gram} \in S} \text{Count}(\text{n-gram})}}_{\substack{\text{fraction of reference n-grams} \\ \text{recovered by the summary}}} \]

Good for “did the summary cover the main points?” Bad for “is the summary well-written?”

Generation 2: Embedding-based Metrics

The core insight: instead of matching surface tokens, match meanings using contextual embeddings.

BERTScore

Embed each token with BERT, then compute token-level cosine similarity between candidate and reference.

\[ R_{\text{BERT}} = \underbrace{\frac{1}{|x|} \sum_{x_i \in x} \max_{\hat{x}_j \in \hat{x}} \mathbf{x}_i^\top \hat{\mathbf{x}}_j}_{\substack{\text{each reference token finds its best} \\ \text{matching candidate token (greedy)}}} \]

Precision, recall, and F1 variants exist. Correlates far better with human judgment than BLEU or ROUGE — but still requires a reference.

Classification Metrics for LLM Tasks

Many political science uses of LLMs reduce to classification: sentiment, topic, stance, policy category, ideology placement.

Metric Formula When to use
Accuracy \((TP+TN)/N\) Balanced classes
Precision \(TP/(TP+FP)\) Cost of false positive high
Recall \(TP/(TP+FN)\) Cost of false negative high
F1 \(2 \cdot \frac{P \cdot R}{P + R}\) Imbalanced, need balance
Macro-F1 mean of per-class F1 Multi-class, care about minority
Cohen’s \(\kappa\) chance-corrected agreement Human-LLM reliability

For inter-coder reliability between human and LLM annotators, Cohen’s \(\kappa\) and Krippendorff’s \(\alpha\) are the disciplinary standard.

Cohen’s Kappa

\[ \kappa = \underbrace{\frac{p_o - p_e}{1 - p_e}}_{\substack{\text{agreement corrected} \\ \text{for chance}}} \]

where \(p_o\) is observed agreement and \(p_e\) is expected agreement by chance.

Interpretation (Landis & Koch 1977)

  • \(\kappa < 0.20\): slight
  • \(0.21\)–\(0.40\): fair
  • \(0.41\)–\(0.60\): moderate
  • \(0.61\)–\(0.80\): substantial
  • \(0.81\)–\(1.00\): almost perfect

A well-specified LLM annotator should achieve \(\kappa \geq 0.60\) against trained human coders to be usable for research.

Perplexity: Intrinsic Generation Quality

For language models, perplexity is the exponentiated average negative log-likelihood.

\[ \text{PPL}(X) = \exp\left\{ -\underbrace{\frac{1}{t} \sum_{i=1}^{t} \log p_\theta(x_i \mid x_{<i})}_{\substack{\text{average per-token} \\ \text{log-likelihood}}} \right\} \]

  • Lower is better. PPL of 1 means the model is certain and correct.
  • Useful for comparing language models on the same tokenizer and dataset.
  • Not comparable across tokenizers — a foundational caveat.

Perplexity evaluates fit to a distribution. It says nothing about whether the model’s outputs are useful or true.

Generation 3: Benchmark Suites

Benchmarks package hundreds or thousands of evaluation tasks into a single score.

Notable Benchmarks in Detail

  • MMLU (Massive Multitask Language Understanding): 57 subjects from elementary to professional. Near-saturated by frontier models.
  • MMLU-Pro: harder replacement, 10-way multiple choice, more reasoning.
  • GPQA Diamond | citation: 198 PhD-level questions Google-proof even for experts.
  • GSM8K / MATH / AIME: math at increasing difficulty.
  • HumanEval / SWE-bench: code generation → real bug-fixing.
  • TruthfulQA: 817 questions designed to elicit common misconceptions.
  • HELM: Stanford’s holistic framework — accuracy, calibration, robustness, fairness, bias, toxicity, efficiency — across scenarios.

A single benchmark is a data point, not a verdict. A model excellent on MMLU may fail on real analytical writing.

Benchmark Contamination

The original sin of modern evaluation: training data leakage.

  • Benchmarks are public. Scraped web training data contains them.
  • Models memorize test answers rather than learning to reason.
  • Observed: model scores 90% on a benchmark version seen in training, 50% on a paraphrased version.

Mitigations

  • Held-out evaluation sets (private test sets, e.g., FrontierMath, SWE-bench Verified held portions).
  • Paraphrased variants and dynamic benchmarks.
  • Temporal validation: evaluate on tasks from after the training cutoff.

Generation 4: Human Preference and LLM-as-Judge

Benchmarks miss what matters most to users: “which response do I prefer?”

Chatbot Arena (LMSYS)

  • Users chat with two anonymous models side by side, vote on preferred response.
  • Pairwise outcomes aggregated via Elo / Bradley-Terry rating.
  • Hundreds of thousands of votes per model.

The Arena Elo has become the de facto leaderboard because it captures real user preferences on real prompts — not curated multiple choice.

Bradley-Terry Model for Pairwise Preferences

\[ P(i \succ j) = \underbrace{\frac{e^{\beta_i}}{e^{\beta_i} + e^{\beta_j}}}_{\substack{\text{logit probability that} \\ \text{model } i \text{ beats model } j}} \]

Fit by maximum likelihood on observed pairwise outcomes. Equivalent to logistic regression with model-pair indicators.

Caveats

  • Style bias: users prefer longer, more confident answers even when wrong.
  • Population bias: who votes? Not a random sample of researchers.
  • Prompt bias: casual prompts dominate; technical prompts under-represented.

LLM-as-Judge

Use a strong LLM (typically GPT-4 or Claude) to grade outputs from weaker models.

Frameworks

  • MT-Bench: 80 multi-turn prompts, GPT-4 scores 1–10.
  • AlpacaEval: pairwise win-rate against a reference model.
  • Arena-Hard: filtered Arena prompts judged by GPT-4.

When it works

  • Scales cheaply to thousands of evaluations.
  • Correlates ~0.8 with human judgments on many tasks.

When it fails

  • Self-preference bias: GPT-4 rates GPT-4 higher.
  • Position bias: first or second option advantaged.
  • Length bias: longer answers preferred.
  • Breaks down on tasks beyond the judge’s own capability.

Mitigating Judge Bias

Practical corrections from the literature:

  1. Randomize position across trials and average.
  2. Use multiple judges (ensemble) including at least one from a different model family.
  3. Calibrate against human gold on a subset.
  4. Provide a rubric — structured criteria beat “which is better?”
  5. Avoid self-evaluation — don’t let a model judge itself.

Tip

For publication-grade research, combine LLM-as-judge for scale with a stratified human-coded subsample to estimate bias and agreement.

Task-Specific Metrics for Social Science

Task Primary metric Secondary
Text classification Macro-F1, Cohen’s \(\kappa\) Per-class P/R
Topic assignment Coherence (\(C_v\), UMass), \(\kappa\) vs human Exclusivity
Ideological scaling Correlation with DW-NOMINATE / expert coding Test-retest
Stance detection Macro-F1, confusion matrix Per-target F1
Summarization Human rating, factuality ROUGE-L
Information extraction Entity-level P/R/F1 Exact match
Question answering Exact match, F1 on token overlap BERTScore

The best evaluation almost always combines an automated metric with a human audit on a stratified subsample.

Part II — Hallucination Detection

What is a Hallucination?

A hallucination is model output that is plausible but not faithful to inputs, training data, or verifiable reality.

The term is contested:

  • Some argue it anthropomorphizes; prefer “confabulation” or “fabrication.”
  • Others distinguish hallucination (perception-like error) from fabrication (generation of false claims).
  • For our purposes: any output that is confidently wrong.

Taxonomy of Hallucinations

Faithfulness vs. Factuality

Two distinct properties, often conflated:

Faithfulness — does the output accurately reflect the input (source document, retrieved context)?

  • Relevant for: summarization, RAG, translation, extraction.
  • Violated by: adding claims not in the source; contradicting the source.

Factuality — does the output accurately reflect the world?

  • Relevant for: open-ended QA, scholarly claims, current events.
  • Violated by: stating incorrect dates, inventing authors, wrong numerical values.

A model can be faithful but not factual (accurately summarizes a document that is itself wrong) or factual but not faithful (adds correct information not in the source).

Why Do LLMs Hallucinate?

Hallucination is a structural feature, not a bug to be patched:

  1. Training objective rewards fluent, plausible continuations — not truth.
  2. No native uncertainty: the model always produces some next token.
  3. Parametric memory is lossy: facts are compressed into weights and can be confidently misremembered.
  4. Distributional shift: questions beyond training distribution produce confident interpolation.
  5. Instruction following: asked for a citation, the model produces something that looks like one.

The standard mitigation insight: separate knowing from asserting. Give the model tools to check itself.

Detection Strategy 1: Self-Consistency

Ask the same question multiple times with non-zero temperature. If answers disagree, the model is uncertain — and likely hallucinating.

SelfCheckGPT (Manakul et al. 2023)

  1. Generate \(N\) samples at temperature \(T > 0\).
  2. For each sentence in the original response, measure agreement with the samples.
  3. High disagreement \(\Rightarrow\) likely hallucination.

\[ S_{\text{hallu}}(s) = \underbrace{1 - \frac{1}{N} \sum_{n=1}^{N} \text{sim}(s, \text{sample}_n)}_{\substack{\text{inconsistency across} \\ N \text{ independent generations}}} \]

Detection Strategy 2: Semantic Entropy

Farquhar et al. (Nature, 2024): cluster sampled outputs by semantic equivalence (using an NLI model), then compute entropy over meanings, not tokens.

\[ H_{\text{sem}} = -\underbrace{\sum_{c \in \text{Clusters}} p(c) \log p(c)}_{\substack{\text{entropy over} \\ \text{semantic clusters}}} \]

  • Resolves the problem that “Paris” and “The capital is Paris” look different at the token level but are the same meaning.
  • Substantially outperforms token-level log-likelihood uncertainty.
  • State-of-the-art among reference-free detectors.

Detection Strategy 3: Retrieval Verification

Ground every claim against an external knowledge source.

FActScore (Min et al. 2023): decompose biography into atomic facts, check each against Wikipedia. Widely used for long-form factuality evaluation.

Detection Strategy 4: Chain-of-Verification

Dhuliawala et al. (2023): have the model verify its own draft.

  1. Produce initial response.
  2. Generate a list of verification questions that, if answered correctly, would confirm the response.
  3. Answer each verification question independently (fresh context, no access to the draft).
  4. Revise the response in light of verification answers.

The independence step is the key — it prevents the model from rubber-stamping its own errors.

Detection Strategy 5: Uncertainty from Logits

When available, raw token probabilities are informative:

  • Mean log-probability of generated tokens — low = uncertain.
  • Minimum token probability — catches single weak links.
  • Predictive entropy per token.
  • Margin between top-1 and top-2 probabilities.

Problems

  • Closed-source models (GPT-4, Claude via API) rarely expose full logits.
  • Well-calibrated on MMLU is not well-calibrated on fabrication.
  • RLHF systematically reduces calibration: the model becomes overconfident.

Evaluation Sets for Hallucination

Benchmark Scope

For political science applications: build your own domain-specific evaluation set. Generic benchmarks do not measure whether a model correctly attributes Supreme Court opinions or reports policy content.

Mitigation: Retrieval-Augmented Generation

The single most effective hallucination reducer in production.

\[ p(y \mid x) = \underbrace{\int p(y \mid x, z) \, p(z \mid x) \, dz}_{\substack{\text{marginalize over} \\ \text{retrieved documents } z}} \]

  • Retrieve top-\(k\) relevant documents, condition generation on them.
  • Require in-text citations to the retrieved documents.
  • Measure faithfulness via NLI between claim and cited document.

RAG reduces but does not eliminate hallucination. Models still:

  • Ignore retrieved context in favor of parametric memory.
  • Confidently paraphrase inaccurately.
  • Cite documents that do not support the claim made.

Mitigation: Abstention and Calibrated Refusal

A model that knows what it does not know is more valuable than one that knows more but asserts it all.

Selective prediction: output a confidence score and abstain below a threshold.

\[ \hat{y} = \begin{cases} \text{prediction} & \text{if } \text{conf} \geq \tau \\ \text{abstain} & \text{otherwise} \end{cases} \]

Evaluate with risk-coverage curves: as the abstention rate increases (lower coverage), accuracy on the answered portion should rise.

A model useful for research should abstain appropriately on questions requiring facts it cannot verify.

A Workflow for Detecting Hallucination in Your Research

Every published finding that relies on LLM-generated text should include an empirical factuality estimate with a confidence interval.

Part III — Model Selection

The Model Selection Problem

There are now dozens of frontier models and hundreds of open-weights models. Which do you use?

The question is not “which is best?” but “which is best for my task, at my budget, with my constraints?”

The Selection Axes

Capability Tiers (as of 2026)

  • Frontier — Claude Opus, GPT-4.x, Gemini 2.x Ultra, o-series reasoners. Best quality, highest cost, typically closed-source.
  • Workhorse — Claude Sonnet, GPT mid-tier, open models 70B–200B. Near-frontier quality on most tasks at ~10× lower cost.
  • Small — Haiku, GPT-mini, Llama/Qwen 7B–13B. Fast, cheap, good for classification, retrieval, short generation.
  • Specialized — code models (Codestral), math (DeepSeek-Math), domain models (Med-PaLM, BloombergGPT).

Rule of thumb for research: prototype on a frontier model, then try to distill or match with a workhorse or small model for the production run.

Open-Weights vs. Closed API

Dimension Closed API (GPT, Claude, Gemini) Open Weights (Llama, Qwen, Mistral, DeepSeek)
Quality ceiling Highest Catching up, sometimes matching
Cost per call Per-token fees Compute cost (often cheaper at scale)
Data privacy Data leaves your institution Can run fully on-premise
Reproducibility Model versions change Weights are fixed artifacts
Fine-tuning Limited, expensive Full control (LoRA, QLoRA)
Regulatory Review every update Pin a version
Speed to ship Instant Infrastructure required

For replicable academic research, open-weights models with a pinned checkpoint are strongly preferred. Closed APIs are moving targets.

Cost Mathematics

\[ \text{Cost}_{\text{total}} = \underbrace{N_{\text{calls}}}_{\substack{\text{number of} \\ \text{API calls}}} \cdot \left( \underbrace{p_{\text{in}} \cdot t_{\text{in}}}_{\substack{\text{input tokens} \\ \times \text{ price}}} + \underbrace{p_{\text{out}} \cdot t_{\text{out}}}_{\substack{\text{output tokens} \\ \times \text{ price}}} \right) \]

Output tokens typically cost 3–5× input tokens. Concrete example:

  • Annotating 10,000 documents at 2K input tokens + 50 output tokens each
  • Frontier pricing (example): $15/1M input, $75/1M output
  • Cost: \(10000 \cdot (15 \cdot 0.002 + 75 \cdot 0.00005) = \$338\)
  • Same on a workhorse at $3/$15: about $68 — a 5× saving.

Cost Reduction Strategies

  1. Prompt caching — charge once per repeated system prompt, not per call.
  2. Batch inference — 50% discounts on major APIs for async workloads.
  3. Smaller models + few-shot — often match larger models on classification.
  4. Distillation — label with the frontier model, fine-tune a small model on the labels.
  5. Structured output — shorter, schema-constrained outputs cost less.
  6. Truncation — do not send the full document if the first 500 tokens suffice.

For large-scale annotation (>10K documents), serious cost engineering can reduce a $5,000 bill to $500 without measurable quality loss.

Latency and Throughput

Two distinct quantities:

  • Latency — time from submission to first (or last) token of a single request.
  • Throughput — tokens per second across a batch of parallel requests.

Research batch jobs prioritize throughput. Interactive applications prioritize latency.

  • For 10K document classification: run in parallel, maximize throughput, use the batch API, wait overnight.
  • For a live policy dashboard: keep a small fast model warm.

Context Window Considerations

Modern models support 128K–2M tokens. But:

  • Quality decays with length. The “needle in a haystack” test is passed by many models, but reasoning over the full context is harder.
  • Cost scales with input tokens. 1M tokens at $3/1M = $3 per call.
  • Latency scales super-linearly with input length.

Decision rule: use long context when the task requires cross-document reasoning. Use retrieval when you can localize the relevant passages.

Fine-tuning Decision Tree

Fine-tune only when prompting, few-shot, and retrieval have all been exhausted. It is the most expensive, least portable option.

Licensing: The Often-Ignored Constraint

Matters especially for publication and replication:

  • Commercial APIs: terms of service usually prohibit training competing models on outputs.
  • Llama family: community license with an attribution and 700M-MAU clause.
  • Apache 2.0 / MIT: most permissive (Mistral, Qwen, Falcon variants).
  • Research-only licenses: fine for the dissertation, not for a product.
  • Output data ownership: who owns the LLM-annotated data? Usually you do; verify.

Important

For IRB and replication packages: document the exact model version, API endpoint, temperature, seed, and license. Models change — reproducibility requires this metadata.

A Selection Framework for Research

Steps 2 and 6 are where most projects fail. A hand-coded evaluation set of even 100 items from your corpus is worth more than any public benchmark.

Pitfalls to Avoid

  1. Leaderboard chasing: the #1 model on MMLU may not be the #1 for your political text.
  2. Single-model lock-in: always build your pipeline to support multiple backends.
  3. No held-out set: never tune prompts on the same data you evaluate on.
  4. Evaluation drift: models update; re-run your evaluation quarterly.
  5. Cost discovery at scale: estimate costs on 1% before running on 100%.
  6. Ignoring variance: run with multiple seeds; a single run is not an estimate.

Political and Social Science Case Studies

  • Congressional speech classification (ideology): small fine-tuned RoBERTa (~125M) competitive with GPT-4 at 1000× lower cost.
  • Open-ended survey coding: frontier model with few-shot achieves \(\kappa \approx 0.75\) vs human; workhorse achieves \(\kappa \approx 0.70\) at 10× lower cost.
  • News article topic detection: any modern model achieves >95% on major categories; differences appear on subtle topics (e.g., constitutional vs. administrative law).
  • Ideological scaling of judges: LLM-derived scales correlate \(r > 0.9\) with Martin-Quinn scores; frontier > workhorse on edge cases.

Lesson: match model size to task difficulty. Do not use a frontier model to classify sentiment.

Putting It Together: The Evaluation Triad

Summary

Evaluation Metrics

  • BLEU/ROUGE for historical comparison, BERTScore for semantic similarity.
  • Classification metrics + \(\kappa\) for annotation tasks.
  • Benchmarks are samples, not verdicts; watch for contamination.
  • LLM-as-judge scales cheaply but is biased — validate against human gold.

Hallucination Detection

  • Self-consistency and semantic entropy for reference-free detection.
  • Retrieval verification and chain-of-verification for factuality.
  • RAG reduces, does not eliminate.
  • Abstention is a feature, not a failure.

Model Selection

  • Match capability tier to task difficulty.
  • Budget for cost, latency, licensing before choosing.
  • Build your own evaluation set on a small gold standard.
  • Reproducibility requires pinned models and documented configurations.
