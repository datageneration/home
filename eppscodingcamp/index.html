EPPS Coding Camp

Preface

EPPS Coding camp is proposed to provide incoming graduate students a basic coding training to help prepare participants to grasp the fundamental aspects of statistical programming and data management for further developments in different disciplines. It is designed for applications across various disciplines but not to replace domain-specific research method courses.

Introduction to the Coding Camp

This camp is designed to equip incoming graduate students from a wide range of disciplines with the essential skills needed in R programming, data management, analysis, and visualization, among others. Over three intensive days, the students will learn the fundamentals of R programming to intermediate skills such as distributed computing, preparing students for the data-driven challenges of in respective fields.

The curriculum is inspired by and in some aspects adapted from the University of Chicago Harris School of Public Policy coding camp, focused on active learning, collaboration (social programming), and computational thinking. This camp is cross-disciplinary in design and aimed at providing students with a solid foundation in data handling and analysis techniques, crucial for your academic and professional growth.

Schedule

Day 1: Getting Started with R and Package Management

  • Morning Session: Introduction to R
    • Overview of R and RStudio
    • Installation of software
    • Basic R syntax and operations
    • Data types and structures
    • Basic operations and functions
  • Afternoon Session: Package Management
    • Introduction to CRAN and GitHub packages
    • Installing and loading packages
    • Managing package dependencies
    • Overview of essential packages for data science

Suggested Reading: - “R for Data Science” by Hadley Wickham & Garrett Grolemund, Chapters 1-3

Day 2: Data Management, Analysis, and Visualization

  • Morning Session: Data Management
    • Reading and writing data
    • Data cleaning and transformation with dplyr
    • Tidying data with tidyr
  • Afternoon Session: Data Analysis & Data Visualization
    • Exploratory data analysis (EDA) techniques
    • Visualizations with ggplot2
    • Best practices for presenting data

Suggested Reading: - “R for Data Science” by Hadley Wickham & Garrett Grolemund, Chapters 5-7, 11-14

Day 3: Advanced Topics in R

  • Morning Session: Function Writing
    • Writing custom functions
    • Debugging and error handling
    • Functional programming concepts
  • Afternoon Session: Distributed Computing
    • Introduction to parallel and distributed computing with R
    • Using R with big data (overview of SparkR, etc.)
    • Case studies and applications

Suggested Reading: - “Advanced R” by Hadley Wickham, Chapters on Functions and Functional Programming - “Mastering Spark with R” by Javier Luraschi, Kevin Kuo, Edgar Ruiz (for distributed computing)

Assessment for Track Placement

At the end of Day 1, participants will undergo a brief assessment covering the basics of R programming and package management. This assessment will help us determine the appropriate track (standard or intermediate) for each student, ensuring personalized learning experiences that cater to individual skill levels.

Homework assignments will be designed for different tracks. Students can choose to more challenging intermediate tasks.

Wrap-Up and Next Steps

On the final day, we will review key concepts, discuss how to continue learning and applying these skills in your disciplines, and provide resources for further exploration. Our goal is for students to leave the camp not only with a solid foundation in coding in general but also with the confidence to apply these new skills to research and learning in own fields of study.

This plan offers a balanced mix of instruction, practical application, and interactive learning opportunities to cater to the diverse backgrounds and interests of graduate students across various disciplines. The suggested readings will provide students further preparations in different disciplines.

Reading list:

For a comprehensive coding camp focusing on R programming and data science, a well-curated list of required texts and additional suggested readings can significantly enhance the learning experience. Below is a structured list to guide students through the camp and beyond, covering introductory topics to more advanced subjects in data science.

Required Texts

These texts form the core learning material for the coding camp, providing essential knowledge and skills in R, data management, analysis, and visualization.

  1. “R for Data Science (https://r4ds.had.co.nz/)” by Hadley Wickham & Garrett Grolemund
    • Covers data import, tidy, transformation, visualization, and modeling in R
    • An indispensable resource for beginners and intermediate users to learn and apply R in real-world data science tasks.
  2. “Advanced R” by Hadley Wickham (https://adv-r.hadley.nz/)
    • Delves into advanced aspects of R programming, including functional programming, object-oriented programming, and performance enhancement techniques.
    • Essential for students interested in deepening their understanding of R and writing more efficient code.

Suggested Reading

These readings are recommended to supplement the required texts, offering broader perspectives on data science, statistical thinking, and specialized applications of R in various domains.

  1. “Data Visualization: A practical introduction (https://socviz.co/)” by Kieran Healy
    • Focuses on the principles of data visualization and how to apply them using ggplot2. A great resource for anyone looking to improve their data presentation skills.
  2. “Text Mining with R: A Tidy Approach (https://www.tidytextmining.com/)” by Julia Silge and David Robinson
    • Introduces text mining and makes it accessible to data scientists and students interested in harnessing the power of textual data in R.
  3. “Mastering Spark with R” by Javier Luraschi, Kevin Kuo, Edgar Ruiz (https://therinspark.com/)
    • For students interested in big data and distributed computing, this book covers how to leverage Spark from R for large-scale data analysis.
  4. “Statistical Inference via Data Science: A ModernDive into R and the Tidyverse (https://moderndive.com/)” by Chester Ismay and Albert Y. Kim
    • Offers an introduction to statistical inference with an emphasis on data science principles and practices, using R and the tidyverse.
  5. “Geocomputation with R (https://r.geocompx.org/)” by Robin Lovelace, Jakub Nowosad, Jannes Muenchow
    • Ideal for students with an interest in GIS, spatial data analysis, and mapping, providing a comprehensive guide to using R for geospatial analysis.

Online Resources

  • RStudio (Posit) Cheat Sheets (https://posit.co/resources/cheatsheets/) Quick reference guides for various R packages and functions.

  • R-Bloggers (https://www.r-bloggers.com/) A blog aggregator that provides articles on a wide range of R-related topics, from tutorials to updates on new packages.

  • CRAN Task Views (https://cran.r-project.org/web/views/) Curated lists of R packages and functions categorized by specific fields and tasks, useful for finding tools for particular data science applications.
