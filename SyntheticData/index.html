Public Opinion and AI: Synthetic Data Approach with Theory and Applications

Karl Ho

University of Texas at Dallas

Abstract


As AI systems grow more capable of simulating human-like text, researchers are increasingly exploring how large language models (LLMs) can generate synthetic public opinion data for research, forecasting, and policy simulation. This talk introduces the theoretical foundations and empirical performance of “silicon sampling” — a method where LLMs generate artificial survey responses conditioned on socio-demographic profiles.

Introduction & Motivation

The Traditional Survey Research Challenge

Current Problems:

  • High costs (>$50 per respondent)
  • Response rate decline (<10% for phone surveys accoding to Pew Research)
  • Demographic bias
  • Logistical complexity
  • Temporal constraints

Emerging Solution:

  • Silicon Sampling: LLMs generating synthetic survey responses

  • Cost reduction by 90%+

  • Instant data collection

  • Demographic coverage

  • Scalable experimentation

What is Silicon Sampling?

“Silicon samples” refer to responses generated by Large Language Models (LLMs) when prompted to adopt various personas defined by demographic and political characteristics (Argyle et al., 2023)

Core Concept: LLMs can simulate human survey responses by:

  1. Adopting demographic personas
  2. Answering survey questions “in character”
  3. Generating population-level response distributions

Systematic Literature Review

Foundational Studies: The Silicon Sampling Pioneer

Argyle et al. (2023) - “Out of One, Many” Political Analysis, 31(3), 337-351

  • First major study introducing “silicon sampling” terminology

  • Used GPT-3 with demographic conditioning

  • Compared against 2012, 2016, 2020 ANES data

  • Key finding: “Information contained in GPT-3 goes far beyond surface similarity. It is nuanced, multifaceted, and reflects the complex interplay between ideas, attitudes, and sociocultural context”

  • Citations: 801 (as of July, 2025, Google Scholar) | ~ 500/year

  • Impact: Established the field of LLM-based survey simulation

Critical Evaluations: The Caution Studies

Bisbee et al. (2023) - “The Perils of Large Language Models” SocArXiv Preprint

  • Critical assessment of ChatGPT 3.5 for survey replacement
  • Used ANES feeling thermometer data
  • Key findings:
    • Less variation in responses than real surveys
    • 48% of regression coefficients significantly different from ANES counterparts
    • Sign of effects flips 32% of the time
  • Warning: Responses from prompted “persona” profiles in ChatGPT produce measures of partisan and racial affective polarization that are seven times larger than the average opinion of humans”

Recent Advances: The Refinement Studies

  • Jiang et al. (2024) - “Donald Trumps in the Virtual Polls” arXiv:2411.01582

    • Cross-cultural validation (US-China comparison)
    • Election prediction capabilities
    • Key contributions:
      • Strong in-sample predictive ability, closely aligning with human responses when demographic inputs are included
      • Model demonstrates potential for out-of-sample forecasting, with accuracy supported when historical voting patterns are incorporated

  • Sun et al. (2024) - “Random Silicon Sampling”

    • Group-level rather than individual-level demographic information
    • Language models can generate response distributions remarkably similar to actual U.S. public opinion polls

Literature Synthesis: Current State (2023-2024)

Promising Evidence:

  • Notable correlation between human responses and LLM-generated ‘silicon samples’
  • Cross-cultural validity in some contexts
  • Cost and speed advantages
  • Scalability for complex scenarios

Persistent Concerns:

  • Performance disparities, especially when comparing countries
  • Biases related to gender, ethnicity, age, education, and social class
  • Variance suppression issues
  • Temporal instability
  • Consensus: Silicon sampling shows promise but requires careful validation and hybrid approaches

Theoretical Foundations

How LLMs “Know” Public Opinion

  • Training Data Sources:
    • News articles on voting patterns
    • Academic research on political behavior
    • Social media political discussions
    • Historical election coverage
    • Polling data and analyses
  • Pattern Recognition Process:
    1. Statistical associations learning
    2. Demographic-opinion mapping
    3. Contextual relationship encoding

Theoretical Mechanisms

  1. Distributional Semantics
  • LLMs learn word co-occurrence patterns
  • “College-educated” + “suburban” + “woman” → Democratic voting probability
  1. Implicit Social Theory
  • Models encode sociological relationships
  • Demographic characteristics → Political attitudes → Behavioral outcomes
  1. Cultural Pattern Matching
  • Ability to capture the complexity of natural language and encode a wide range of human experiences, cultural norms, and decision-making patterns

Assumptions & Limitations

  • Core Assumptions:

    1. Training data represents population accurately
    2. Demographic stereotypes have predictive validity
    3. Text patterns reflect genuine attitudes

  • Key Limitations:

    1. Static Knowledge: Training cutoff dates
    2. Demographic Reductionism: Complex individuals → Simple categories
    3. Cultural Bias: Models perform better in Western, English-speaking, and developed nations

Methodological Approaches

Silicon Sampling Workflow

  • Steps:

    1. Demographic Specification: Age, gender, education, location, etc.
    2. Prompt Engineering: “I am a 45-year-old college-educated white woman from suburban Ohio…”
    3. Response Generation: Multiple iterations for variance
    4. Statistical Comparison: Chi-square, KL-divergence, correlation analysis

Prompt Engineering Strategies

Basic Persona Prompting:

"I am a 35-year-old Black male 
lawyer from Phoenix. I am an 
atheist. Who did I vote for 
in 2020?"

Enhanced Context Prompting:

"Based on polling data showing 
college graduates voted 55% for 
Biden, predict this person's vote: 
[demographic description]"

Interview Simulation:

Interviewer: "Thank you for 
participating. Could you tell 
me about your voting choice?"

Respondent: "As a 35-year-old..."

Issue-Based Conditioning:

"Consider this person's likely 
positions on healthcare, climate, 
and economy, then predict their 
vote choice..."

Evaluation Metrics

  • Statistical Measures:

    1. Mean Absolute Error (MAE): Average difference in response distributions
    2. Kullback-Leibler Divergence: Information-theoretic distance measure
    3. Chi-Square Tests: Statistical significance of distribution differences
    4. Regression Coefficient Comparison: Structural relationship preservation

  • Qualitative Assessments:

    • Cross-cultural validity
    • Temporal stability
    • Prompt sensitivity analysis
    • Demographic subgroup performance

Empirical Findings & Performance

Performance by Demographics

Based on systematic analysis across studies:

Education High College vs. non-college well differentiated

Race/Ethnicity Variable Biases related to ethnicity documented

Cross-Cultural Validation

  • Jiang et al. (2024) Findings:

    • US Performance: Strong alignment with ANES data
    • China Performance: LLM effectively replicates cultural differences in values between the U.S. and China on most items
    • Caveat: Occasionally overestimates socially progressive views, particularly in the U.S.

  • Global Pattern (from multinational studies):

    • Models perform better in Western, English-speaking, and developed nations, notably the United States

Accuracy Benchmarks

  • Strong Performance:

    • Mean response alignment: 85-95%
    • Demographic gradients preserved
    • Cultural difference detection
    • Large-scale pattern matching
  • Persistent Issues:
    • Variance suppression (30-50% less variation)
    • Extreme opinion underrepresentation
    • 48% of regression coefficients differ significantly
    • Temporal instability over months

Challenges & Limitations

Variance Suppression Problem

  • The Issue: There is less variation in responses than in the real surveys
  • Human Surveys:
    • High individual variation
    • Unexpected responses
    • Contradictory attitudes
    • Measurement error
  • Silicon Samples:
    • Compressed around stereotypes
    • Overly consistent responses
    • Reduced individual differences
    • “Too perfect” patterns
  • Implication: Statistical inference becomes unreliable

Demographic Bias & Misalignment

  • Documented Biases:

    1. Western/WEIRD Bias: Performance disparities, especially when comparing countries
    2. Progressive Overestimation: Occasionally overestimates socially progressive views
    3. Polarization Amplification: Seven times larger measures of partisan and racial affective polarization
    4. Intersectionality Gaps: Poor handling of multiple demographic identities

Reproducibility Crisis

Temporal Instability: The same prompt yields significantly different results over a 3-month period

Prompt Sensitivity: Minor wording changes cause major distribution shifts

Model Updates: Backend algorithm changes invalidate previous results

Solution: Version control, prompt standardization, ensemble approaches

Ethical Considerations

Privacy & Consent: No human consent for synthetic personas

Stereotype Reinforcement: Risk of amplifying demographic biases

Misrepresentation: Users should ensure that the generated content is not used to mislead or deceive others

Democratic Implications: Potential manipulation of perceived public opinion

How ChatGPT/LLMs Make Political Predictions

  1. Training Data Patterns (Not “Guessing”)

  • LLMs learn from massive datasets that include:

    • News articles about voting patterns by demographics
    • Academic research on political behavior
    • Polling data and election analyses
    • Social media discussions about politics
    • Historical election coverage

  • The model identifies statistical associations like:

    • “College-educated suburban women tend to vote Democratic”
    • “Evangelical protestants have high turnout rates”
    • “Young voters have lower turnout”

How ChatGPT/LLMs Make Political Predictions

  1. Pattern Matching Process

  • When you give it a demographic description, the LLM:

    • Identifies key demographic markers
    • Recalls learned associations from training data
    • Combines multiple signals (age + education + religion + location)
    • Outputs a prediction based on these combined patterns

How ChatGPT/LLMs Make Political Predictions

  1. Limitations of Current Approach

    • Stereotyping: Relies on broad demographic generalizations
    • Temporal drift: Training data may reflect older patterns
    • Context insensitive: Doesn’t account for specific local factors
    • No real survey data: Only using demographic proxies

How to Improve Predictions with Survey Data

  • incorporate actual survey data to enhance predictions
  • train better with local data

How LLMs Make Predictions vs. How You Can Improve Them

  • Current LLM Method:

    • Pattern Recognition: LLMs learn associations from training data like “college-educated suburban women → Democratic voting”
    • Demographic Stereotyping: Combines multiple demographic signals No Real-Time Data: Uses historical patterns, not current survey data

How LLMs Make Predictions vs. How You Can Improve Them

  • Ways to Enhance with Survey Data:

    1. Contextual Prompting
    • Instead of: “Predict who they voted for”, “Based on recent polls showing college graduates voted 55% for Biden…”
    1. Training Examples
    • Provide actual survey responses as examples:
    • “Example: 45-year-old college-educated suburban woman voted Biden”
    1. Issue-Based Predictions
    • Incorporate issue positions from your survey:
      • “Gun rights supporters tend to vote Republican based on our data…”
    1. Validation Against Real Data
    • Compare baseline vs. survey-enhanced predictions
    • Measure accuracy against known outcomes
    • Analyze prediction stability

Appendix: Technical Implementation

Sample Code for Silicon Sampling:

# Generate synthetic survey responses
silicon_sample <- llm_generate(
  demographics_df,
  input = "demographic_description", 
  output = "vote_choice",
  prompt = "I will give you demographic information. 
           Predict who they voted for in 2020. 
           Say 'Trump' or 'Biden' only.",
  model = "gpt-3.5-turbo",
  max_tokens = 5,
  temperature = 0.7
)

# Validate against human survey
validation_results <- compare_distributions(
  silicon_sample$vote_choice,
  human_survey$vote_choice,
  demographic_vars = c("age_group", "education", "race")
)
